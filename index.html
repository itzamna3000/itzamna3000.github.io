About Me

I am a current Adjunct Assistant Research Scientist in the Department of Engineering at Johns Hopkins University. I have worked in magnetic resonance imaging science since 2013. I have experience working with healthy adults, twins, and neurodegenerative geriatric patients. I have oriented my research towards understanding the neural basis of time processing, how this behavior deviates in parkinsonian patients, and if their proficiency lays on genetic or environmental traits. I draw on psychophysics to describe and evaluate the performance of individuals while executing perceptual and motor timing tasks and the effect of intensive training on their behavior. Secondly, with the MRI as the main technological instrument, I explore functional images in the search of activity changes that could explain the effect of training on the timed behavior of healthy humans. Using dimensionality reduction analysis, I aim to identify structures that have a pattern of activation that correlates with behavioral changes and could explain the changes in performance. With the aid of machine learning and linear models, I look for structural patters in the gray and white matter to elucidate the neurobiological substrates that could explain the behavioral variance measure in the behaving humans. Also, by using the additive genetic and environmental models, behavioral and image data, I try to answer the long-asked question if proficiency and easiness to highly performed in timed behavior, in the hundreds of milliseconds, is govern by genetic or environmental factors. Lastly, Parkinson's disease is known to be a neurodegenerative pathology that has deleterious effects on motor behavior and, more importantly, on timed movements. I evaluate the kinetic parameters of walking and the accuracy and precision of steps of parkinsonian patients to assess if intense perceptual training could improve their gait and quality of life.
